# Hiphop MV Continuity

Use this reference when the user wants hiphop music-video feeling rather than brand campaign stillness.

## Core Direction

The user's hiphop work should feel like a controlled music video: beat-driven, performance-led, street-space aware, and edited with motion continuity. It should not feel like static fashion campaign frames placed in a row.

## Face Identity Is Non-Negotiable

For supplied model references:

- Front shots must preserve the exact reference face, hairstyle, skin tone, age, body proportion, and expression temperature.
- Back shots must preserve hairstyle from behind, neck/shoulder shape, stance, garment proportion, and body silhouette.
- If a face becomes a generic fashion model or a different person, the shot fails even if the lighting looks good.
- Prefer medium and medium-wide shots over close-ups when identity preservation is fragile.
- Add negatives for: face swap, generic model face, beautified replacement face, different ethnicity, different age, different hairstyle, face averaging, merged identities.

## MV Motion Beats

Each shot needs a simple action with a clear start and finish:

- walk-in: character enters frame or takes two slow steps toward camera
- lean-in: character pushes off a wall and settles into rhythm
- head-nod beat: two subtle nods matched to beat
- shoulder bounce: restrained rhythm in the torso, not full dance
- chain touch: one controlled touch and release
- turn-back: back-facing model gives a slight head turn, then faces away again
- step-through: camera tracks as the model crosses a doorway, rail, or puddle
- crew reveal: foreground model moves aside or camera slides to reveal others
- orbit: camera arcs around one or two models while they hold rhythm

Avoid static "standing portrait" shots unless they are used as a deliberate final freeze.

## Shot-To-Shot Flow

Plan every shot with:

1. Entry state: where the body begins.
2. Beat action: what moves during the shot.
3. Exit state: what position can cut into the next shot.

Good continuity examples:

- back-view stillness -> head turn -> cut to front-facing nod
- solo walk-in -> match cut to another model stepping through puddle
- hand touches chain -> cut to another model's hand in pocket
- camera slide past a pillar -> cut to group reveal from behind the next pillar
- four-person crew nod -> cut to back-view final silhouette

## Camera Movement For Hiphop MV

Use controlled movement that feels musical:

- low-angle handheld push-in with subtle body sway
- lateral tracking along a wall or railing
- slow orbit around two people
- short whip-pan or motion-blur transition only between shots, not inside every shot
- locked final frame only at the ending

Avoid:

- every shot locked on tripod
- every shot slow push-in
- fast random camera shake
- excessive fashion-commercial slow motion
- movement that does not match body rhythm

## Environment Realism

The location should feel like a real shootable underground/industrial site, not an AI stage:

- practical lights should have visible sources or believable off-screen direction
- puddles should sit in cracks and low points, not cover the whole floor like a mirror
- haze should reveal light cones, not cover the subject
- wall damage should follow believable surfaces: water streaks under pipes, chipped paint around edges, worn railings where hands touch
- repeat environment anchors across shots: same shutter type, same rail color, same cold tube light, same blue-orange practical spill

## Recommended Sequence Logic

For four models:

1. A back-view silhouette with a slow head nod.
2. Match cut to D front walk-in or shoulder bounce.
3. Cut to B crossing frame or leaning off a shutter.
4. Cut to C back-view walk-through corridor.
5. A+D interaction with camera orbit.
6. B+C interaction with one controlled gesture.
7. Crew reveal with all four in frame.
8. Final back-view or low-angle crew freeze.

This gives the piece musical progression instead of a series of unrelated still poses.
